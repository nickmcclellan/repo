by:  Nick McClellan

 

Simple

 

            It’s dark where I am now.  Huddled up in a corner, I feel safe.  I’m on an uncomfortable red bench a few feet from the front employee door.  It’s locked and the alarm is on.  Its light indicator blinks in the darkness, next to the computer at the Employee Service Center—ESC as we commonly refer to it.  Donna, the “Leader-on-Duty”, or LOD for short, glances up from the ESC away from whatever menial overnight task she’s engaged.  Most likely she’s dividing her team amongst the store—having one group of people focus on emptying the truck while the other group focuses on running freight.  My supervisor Ed glares at me as he walks to the control room, probably off to print some of the remaining labels we have left for the night.  Ed’s probably timing this, I think, pushing the green “call” button on my cell phone anyways as he rushes past Donna, secluding himself into the room at the end of the hall.

The phone is ringing.  Each bland tone feeds the fear that I have woken his parents.  This warming piece of technology against my ear—I listen intently, counting the rings.  An answer; I feel warm—almost loved, but knowing it’s too soon.  He’s bored.  He went to his grandmother’s house today.  She’s growing old and senile; she misses him, and wants to spend more time with him.  He’s preoccupied though, but not really.  He told me he walked around the house for hours, mad with boredom, finally settling at his piano where he practiced for a few hours.  I wondered to myself if he played my song.

            It’s 1:21 now.  Fifteen minute breaks aren’t this long and I’m sure Ed will point this out to me when I return to the floor to mark down clearance merchandise.  Time is a duplicitous snake, I decide.  The way time works with him, the length of a minute seems to shorten, and time steals him away from me.  No longer his, I must now return to work, leaving my weary guy to his dreams.  I probably shouldn’t call him my guy.  It’s too soon.

It’s an easy night tonight.  We’ve only got a few hundred units to scan and we’ll wrap that up pretty quickly.  Zipping through a rack of women’s clearance clothing, I grab tags, physically a machine, but mentally someplace far less methodical.  It’s only a short while after returning to work that I find that making it to sunrise is going to take a while, especially now since my perk of the night has come and gone.

3 a.m. comes around and you’ll find me stocking back in the Trim.  Visibly disheveled, I walk wearily to the front of the store and into the break room, covered in the pieces of loose cardboard that have found their way to my shirt from the boxes and boxes of holiday themed decoration.  After stuffing thousands of gift bags onto aisles bleeding with “holiday cheer,” I sit at a lunch table, staring into cell phone screen.  I type feverishly some sort of meaningful message, a “thank you”, a “miss you”—something.  Anything. 

Lunch consists of two peanut butter sandwiches and a small bottle of Gatorade.  My co-workers marvel at the consistency of the lunches I bring, almost heckling me for my predictable behavior.  Simplicity:  it’s something that I find I need.  I watch some random late night movie on TBS as the early morning flow team walks by the break room, greeting me on their way to the backroom.  I’m in high spirits; the sun is coming soon and I’ll be able to go home and get my sleep.  Wiping my lips with the paper towel I stuffed with my sandwiches earlier in the evening, I trash the plastic sandwich bags and return to Trim.

Trim sucks.  Condensing an aisle of ornaments seems to sooth my wandering imagination, as endless wonderings are the only things that get me through the morning.  A parade of potential emotions dance through my head, keeping my mind off of the boredom into which I have sunken.  He is spinning around me, in my head, at every moment, at every second, I can’t stop, though I want to.  I smile, for a moment not caring, staring at package of holiday cards, thinking of him and his words to me hours earlier. 

In my head, I can envision that wonderful day.  January 5th represents for me so much.  I can return to him, go back to school, and we no longer have to endure this meaningless separation.  In my heart, I feel like I deserve this happiness.  I feel like when the world is handed to me, I will take it and it will be unambiguously mine!  No apologies, no struggle, and no pain.  I’m to atone for nothing.

Some of my co-workers decide to leave early and I do as well.  I slip out of the employee door, now unlocked and unsecured, and jump into my car, bundled in a light jacket and anxious to get home.  The drive home is livened by one song.  “Fight Test” plays over and over again.  I linger on the words:

 

I don’t know where the sunbeams and and the starlight begins

It’s all a mystery…

 

I feel like I too can’t understand this concept of night and day.  I haven’t seen the sun for days so this sense of confusion descends upon me.  I ponder the whole concept as I drive across Highway 41, approaching Kennesaw Mountain.

Melissa, a friend of mine on the flow team, asked me if I got much sleep yesterday.  This struck me because, to my perspective, I hadn’t slept yesterday; I slept earlier in the evening.  She was right though, while working overnight, this night becomes yesterday and today.  However, to me the day had not become tomorrow.  I feel like she should have asked, "Did you get much sleep today?"  That's the most interesting thing about working nights—realizing just how short days are.  For everyone else it's like you go to sleep, and then you wake up, and its tomorrow.  It seems like this immeasurable spance of time exists from the moment you close your eyes to the moment you awaken—in these precious hours, a day concludes and a new one begins.  For me, however, it seems the days never end.  I go to sleep in the morning and wake up in the afternoon and it’s the same day.  I got to work and come home in the morning and it seems that the day never changed.  I realize tomorrow doesn’t follow sleep.  When I'm at home, now, it seems tomorrow never comes. 

The road is empty and these streets are long.  I watch early morning commuters flash by in the left lane.  For a moment, I laugh at their pointless rush.  I am happy this is the end for me; my time in this day is over before it’s even begun.  “Fight Test” repeats four or five times before I pull into my driveway and dart into my house.  I notice the sky brighten, the horizon almost accentuated by that orb of light, but I miss it.  I slipe into my house, avoiding it.  Like a vampire, I lay inside my concealed space, untouched by the sun.

            My room is secluded, unexposed to the world outside, like a cave.  Over my window I have draped the sheets to my dorm room bed, effectively covering my window and blotting out the dawn.  I sit at my computer checking mail and seeking any friend that might be awake at this early hour.  My cat purrs on the bed while squinting her eyes.  She propositions me to come pet her—I soon oblige. 

My computer fan ceases humming and the screen goes black.  I rub her fur softly, rhythmically.  She outstretches her paws, separating her tiny kitty fingers.  I rub her kitty palm, sliding my cheek against her thin, small body.  She loves this.  I carry her to the cat food in the kitchen and leave her chomping on the counter.

Closing the door behind me, I instinctively lock it.  Seeing almost a shimmer of sunlight peek through the edges of the sheet on my window, I shut off the light and hop into bed.  I collect as many blankets as possible to cover me, to warm me through the morning and hopefully into noon—assuming I don’t kick them from the bed.  I lean over to my tabletop fan, turning it on.  Sleep feels so good, I think to myself; it’s all I’ve got.  The fan turns softly as I drift to sleep.

            This separation will end soon.  This world is so relentless and dark.  I need to go back, back to him, back to it all.  Certainly I deserve this.  Things won’t be easy, but I’ll have someone to go home to, someone to soldier through for.  This time, it will be easier; I won’t have to worry about anything because I’ve found him.  I desperately want to do this for him, I want to be for him a person to be trusted—God, maybe even a person to be loved.  I want to be something for someone, at last!  I can do it; I understand how this is done.  It seems so insane, but I know this is mine.  I can have this.

 

            I wake in late afternoon to my cell phone calling.  I reach over, realizing it is a text message.  I scroll to my inbox and read his response from my earlier message before.

            “I miss the hell out of you.”

            Closing my eyes while pressing the phone into my chest, I grin.  This is mine.

 

Separation

 

            All I see are pieces—shattered pieces, all around me.  I feel like a gust of wind has blown over me and I’ve nothing to protect me from the cold.  It’s inevitable.  This pain, this drop, this loss, it all comes and goes.  It’s coming now.

What is separation?  Is it more than just a formality, the task before me?  What is my purpose now?  I feel like these pieces at my feet are large and tangible, that maybe I can work with this.  I can rebuild him.  He can tell me something different.  He doesn’t have to be so destructive, does he?  He doesn’t have to hurt me this time.

Is this what I deserve?  Do I deserve to fight this all alone?  Is this some test?  The questions spin around me and I can’t take it.  Jumping from my bed, I shut off the light.  I lie in my bed and I let it come.  I let this misery come over me and I am at its mercy.

What is it anyways?  What makes you feel so god-awful when someone you care about tells you that he or she can’t be what they said they’d be?  I was fine before him, nothing much has changed since him.  I really only lost a good weekend activity.  I suppose movie nights spooning on his bed are out of the question.  I still have my friends.  She’s on her way, Missy is.  She’s on here way to be here for me; but what can she do?  What can I do?  I close my eyes again and let it come…

I called him and asked him about it.  Things had been different since we got back, I could sense that.  I expected so much and got so little.  He paused when I asked the question and it all came crashing down.  I knew.  Like a house of cards I had built on his motionless body, he was gracefully still.  I had to disturb that, destroying my creation by questioning it.  I wouldn’t build this again, I can’t.

He called again, I’m not sure why.  I seem to remember some glimmer of hope, some maybe, some splitting of the blame, some meaningless shit like that, but I wasn’t buying anymore.  He’d changed; he obviously wasn’t there when I needed him most.  Closing my eyes, that’s what hurt the most.  That’s what I was dying to forget.  This test was over.

I’m not paranoid.  If anything, this has proven to me just how perceptive I am.  He tore through my willful subterfuge, destroying my blind trust, allowing me to see—to finally feel what it was I was holding.  I could feel it come over me now, this light, this truth, this self-awareness that I was alone.  I was too stupid to realize I was tuning out the truth.  Why did I have to be so willingly blind?  He tossed a stone through my house of mirrors and it all came crashing down before me.

I’m back to pieces now.  Staring at this mess, I wonder if there is some new form amidst the chaos.  I just don’t care now, though.  I can’t find words, I can’t find meaning.

 

Going Through the Motions

 

            I was sitting on the bus today and Brandi got on and started up a conversation.  She sits next to me in my Spanish class which we both had recently departed from.  She was surprised to see me on the 14 bus on the way to her apartment.  She wondered if I lived there.  I told her I didn’t.  We talked about class.  I informed her I lived on campus.  We pulled up to my stop and I gave her a “have a nice day” or something, and I walked off.

            I went into my room and shut the door.  I am surprised my roommate didn’t show up for that pitiful display yesterday, but I’m grateful he didn’t.  I was really very upset.  You remember—pieces, no meaning—or should I say lack of definition?  If there is one thing I can communicate that’s meaningful right now…I don’t know what it is.

            I shut the door.  I toss my bag on the floor and turn on my computer.  Missy’s box pops up on my computer and I write to her.  We’re going to see a movie tonight.  Signs.  I hear it’s scary.  We’ve never seen it.  I can’t think of anything else to do on a Friday night.  It’s nice to have my good friend with me during quite possibly the worst weekend this year.

            Before walking out the door, I notice that my photo of Shirley Manson had fallen off the wall.  It was fastened to the wall by a sticker strip and a small plastic hook.  The photo was secured in a frame with two large rectangular pieces of glass and condensed cardboard along with four small metal fasteners.  It was rather heavy, especially the glass covering the photo.  I remember last night I heard the frame come crashing down.  In the morning, I noticed that the sticker strip had been weighted so much by the frame it started wrenching a bubble in the paint on the wall.  I star at the ugly scar on the wall, the imperfection—the disruption.  I scan the wall, lingering on the one blemish.  It’s all I seem to notice, a gaping white spot on my wall with this bubble—this immovable destruction now present forever before my eyes.  Shaking my head, I grab my jacket and head for the door, trying to ignore it.

            Music seems very meaningless these days.  Driving to the video store, we listen to a mixed Spring Break CD I made last year.  Missy puts the top down and we begin singing out loud, smiling—reveling our evening escape.  It feels good.  If for just a moment, I don’t recall what it was I was so broken up about.  I see her to the left of me.  She’s absolutely the most beautiful thing I’ve laid my eyes on.  The wind flies through her hair, and she’s feeling fine.  I speculate that an elevated sense of meaning must envelope her.  She remains untouched and free.  Unblemished.  She flies down the road so fast, belting out the B-52’s, without shame.  She’s here with me.  Me.  Her best friend. 

My eyes return to the floor briefly to grab another CD from her CD case—the floor.  Blinking, I swallow as it rises in my belly.  Somehow I’m reminded the absence, realizing that he’s not coming back.  I’m sick of in all.  Back and forth—living life between distractions.  At the video store, I grab the DVD from the shelf and hurry to the front.  I pay on my credit card.  I usually do.  Missy never pays.  I should probably talk to her about it.  I walk out the door to her car, staring into unreality.

This movie scares me, but I think it’s just creepy.  It plays off of childhood fears; I was afraid of aliens when I was younger.   I always thought they were somewhere in the darkness, outside my window perhaps.  People called them Grays.  They have large eyes and very thin bodies.  I imagine they are very light.  They represent to me this unknown force that is mysterious and powerful, and it scares me, even today.  I remember thinking, when I was younger, “How can I live on my own?”  Certainly, living on my own would require me having to sleep in the dark—alone.  Is that when they’ll get me?  I think of this as I see the alien’s shadows darken a window in the movie.

I ask Missy to lay with me during the final scene.  It’s very scary; it’s nice having her around like that—having anyone around like that.  I hold her.  It’s empty, though.  She smiles, but she can’t see.  She can’t see that I’m not even there.

The movie ends, but it’s still early in the evening.  We decide to venture over to Sonya’s for an evening of fun.  I grab the bottle of Bacardi my sister left me last weekend thinking I’ll probably need it.  We giggle, like children with fire.

I’ve never drowned my sorrows in alcohol before, but I figure, why not try?  The first shot is painful, I cringe and cough, Missy teases me.  Sonya and Ilya look on like selfish cats, sipping on their vodka tonics like they were saucers of milk.  They’re in love, or so it would seem.  They sort of started things when, you know, I started things with—what’s his name?  Anyways, they got all “gung-ho” like me and it was good, for a while, it was really nice.  We all had someone, and we all were having a good time.  I was happy that we were with people and we could still come together as friends and have a good time.

That changed though.  Sonya and Ilya remain together and now I’m, well, I’m nothing.  I observe them, thinking, “This isn’t natural.  How can they be together like this?  They barely know each other…”  I drown the questions with a shot of raspberry twist.  Missy giggles, taking her shot.  This one is so much for her that she stumbles, slamming her heel into my toe.  I shriek out, Missy laughs.  Boys shouldn’t make that sound.  We laugh, falling on the floor.  It feels good, I feel so good with her.  She smiles, feeling even more that she’s done her part for me.

That changed though.  I feel like I’m just dancing around this great stage, stepping on toes, stumbling and getting up.  I would like to think I don’t see the large hole in the ground before me, but I do—I cringe when I see it, sliding past it, avoiding it at all costs.  I would like to think that I won’t, at any moment, fall into it.  It’s there, I can’t help it.  Every time I feel better, another piece of my world falls into it.  I feel like I’m about to as well.

Minutes later, I have.  Laying on Sonya’s futon, the lights off, I can hear Missy, Sonya, and Ilya talking about me upstairs.  “What’s his problem…Why doesn’t he get over this?” They don’t understand.  It’s not about him anymore—I assure myself—it’s about me.  It’s about what I can’t see anymore and what was never there to begin with.  What does it matter if I get drunk?  What does it matter if I read all this meaningless bullshit in class?  How does it change me?  Why is it that I have to deal with these pieces that don’t fit, that seem like they’ve never fit?  I can’t deal with this now, I want to go back about 5 or 6 years and start over again, but I can’t.  I can’t do it, I can’t deal.  I’m lost, hopelessly lost.  I don’t know how or why I’m going to make it though—how I’m going to find my way—but it’s coming.  It’s coming, and I can’t stop it.

 

A month later, I’m driving back from some coffee shop, pulling into my deck.  “Fight Test” fumes out of my car speakers.  It’s been a while since I’ve really listened to it.  The sun is out; I can see that—I no longer sleep in the shadows of day.  I don’t understand the significance of this song, but I sing it out and it’s nice.  This song brings it back, every bit of it, whole and in my hands.  Pulling into my parking spot, the Lips sing one more verse for me, the last note echoing…

 

The test is over…NOW!

 

I turn the key, yanking it from the ignition.  Staring at the steering column, I close my eyes, saying the one meaningful thing I can hope to communicate here.  I say it clearly and with everything bit of conviction I have left in me.

“Asshole.”
