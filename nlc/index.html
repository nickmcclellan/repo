City Fiscal Conditions in 2013

The nation’s city finance officers report that the fiscal condition of cities in 2013 is improving, although they are continuing to confront the prolonged effects of the economic downturn. Recovering local and regional economies experiencing slowly improving housing markets and increased consumer spending are strengthening local tax bases and economic outlooks.

Below are highlighed data from the 2013 survey indicating the cautious approach many cities are taking in regards to budgets and expenditures amid a slowly improving economy.

Revenue and Spending Trends

Finance officers projected a small year-over-year increase in general fund revenues measured in inflation-adjusted dollars — the first increase since 2006.

Tax Revenues

Property tax revenues continue to decline in 2012 and are projected to decline in 2013.

Sales tax and local income tax revenues experienced marked increases in 2012 with projects for further growth in 2013

Factors Influencing City Budgets

Leading the list of factors that finance officers say have increased over the previous year are health benefit costs and pension costs.

In a shift from prior years, more city finance officers report increases in the local tax base and the overall health of the local economy.

  • Increased impact
  • Decreased impact

Health benefits, pension, and infrastructure costs were reported as negative impacts on city budgets, among several other factors that rated highly.

Few factors had a decreased impact on the cities' ability to meet their budget needs, but respondents indicated that decreased support from state and federal funds had an impact.

Revenue Actions and Spending Cuts

The most common action taken to boost city revenues has been to increase the amount of fees charged for services — two in five city finance officers reported that their city had raised fees.
